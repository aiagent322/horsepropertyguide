Can You Haul Water for Horses Legally?

Hauling water for horses is legal in Arizona and is a common practice in rural areas where wells are not present, well yields are insufficient, or properties lack utility water connections. There are no state or county regulations that prohibit a landowner from transporting potable or non-potable water to a property for livestock watering purposes.

Water is typically purchased from municipal fill stations, private water hauling companies, or agricultural water suppliers, transported in food-grade tanks, and stored in above-ground holding tanks or cisterns on the property. The legality of water hauling itself is not in question, but the adequacy of hauled water as the sole water source may affect permitting and financing.

Some counties require a demonstrated adequate water supply as a condition of building permits for residential structures, and hauled water may not satisfy this requirement for residential construction. For lenders, properties relying exclusively on hauled water can present collateral concerns, particularly if the absence of a well or utility water connection limits the property's market appeal and appraised value.

Above-ground water storage tanks on horse property may require setback compliance and in some cases a permit depending on tank size and whether the installation involves grading or foundation work. Buyers should confirm that hauled water will meet their anticipated daily horse water requirements before relying on it as a permanent solution.

Water Demand Calculations for Hauled Water Horse Operations

Calculating water demand accurately is essential for buyers evaluating a hauled water horse property. Horses require approximately 10 to 15 gallons per day under normal conditions, with consumption rising to 20 or more gallons per day during Arizona's summer heat when temperatures regularly exceed 105 degrees. A small operation with four horses requires a minimum of 40 to 60 gallons per day for livestock, plus residential household demand of approximately 80 to 120 gallons per day for a typical family. Combined daily demand of 120 to 180 gallons per day translates to 840 to 1,260 gallons per week — meaning delivery or fill station visits at least weekly for a 1,000-gallon storage tank.

Buyers should evaluate the storage capacity on the property against their calculated demand and assess the logistics of regular water procurement. Fill station locations, delivery service availability in the specific area, water cost per gallon, and the reliability of delivery during peak demand periods — including Arizona's monsoon season when access roads can become impassable — all affect the practical feasibility of a hauled water operation. Properties that appear viable for hauled water in dry conditions may face supply challenges during summer storms or when delivery services are overwhelmed by regional demand.

Long-Term Water Strategy for Hauled Water Properties

Buyers purchasing a hauled water horse property should develop a long-term water strategy before closing. The three primary options are to continue hauling water indefinitely, to drill a well if subsurface water is accessible, or to pursue a municipal water connection if service is available within a reasonable distance. Well feasibility depends on the depth to groundwater, the expected yield based on neighboring well logs available through the Arizona Department of Water Resources, and the cost of drilling and equipping a well at that depth. In some parts of Arizona, well drilling costs are prohibitive because water is deep or unreliable. In others, a productive well can be developed for $20,000 to $40,000 and eliminate ongoing hauling costs.

The Arizona Department of Water Resources maintains a well registry database that is publicly accessible and contains information about existing wells in any geographic area, including their depth, yield, and water quality. Buyers evaluating hauled water properties should review neighboring well data before purchase to assess well feasibility. The presence of productive neighboring wells at reasonable depths is a strong indicator that well development is viable. The absence of nearby wells, or the presence of only deep low-yield wells, suggests that hauled water may be the long-term solution regardless of cost.

Key Takeaways

  • Hauling water for horses is legal throughout Arizona with no regulatory prohibition.
  • Counties may not accept hauled water as an adequate supply for residential building permits.
  • Lenders may discount property value when hauled water is the only available source.
  • Above-ground storage tanks may require setback compliance and permits depending on size.
