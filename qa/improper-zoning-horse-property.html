What Happens If Your Horse Property Is Not Properly Zoned?

Operating horse-keeping or equestrian facilities on improperly zoned property exposes owners to several enforcement consequences that can be costly and difficult to resolve. County or municipal code enforcement agencies investigate zoning complaints and, upon confirming a violation, issue a notice of violation requiring the property owner to come into compliance within a specified period.

Failure to comply results in daily fines that accumulate until the violation is abated. In serious cases involving commercial operations on residential zoning, enforcement can include cease and desist orders, injunctions, and court action.

For buyers who purchase horse property in good faith and later discover the property is not legally zoned for equestrian use, the remedies are limited and expensive. Options include applying for a variance, requesting a zoning reclassification, applying for a conditional use permit if the use is eligible, or discontinuing the equestrian use entirely.

None of these remedies is guaranteed. Zoning reclassification requires approval from the county board of supervisors or planning commission and may face opposition from neighboring landowners. A variance requires demonstrated hardship and is not routinely granted. Buyers who discover a zoning violation after closing have recourse against the seller only if the seller made affirmative misrepresentations about the property's legal status. Conducting zoning due diligence before purchase — not after — is the only reliable way to avoid this outcome.

Key Takeaways

  • Zoning violations result in notices of violation, daily fines, and potential court action.
  • Remedies including variances and reclassification are expensive, time-consuming, and not guaranteed.
  • Buyers have limited post-closing recourse unless the seller made affirmative zoning misrepresentations.
  • Pre-purchase zoning verification with the county planning department is the only reliable protection.
