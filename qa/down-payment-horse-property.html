How Much Down Payment Is Required for Horse Property?

Down payment requirements for horse property depend on the loan product, the property's classification, and the lender's risk assessment. For conventional loans on owner-occupied horse property classified as residential, Fannie Mae guidelines allow down payments as low as 5 percent for primary residences, though properties with complex characteristics — significant acreage, equestrian outbuildings, rural location — typically trigger lender overlays requiring 10 to 20 percent. Most lenders working in equestrian markets require 20 percent down on horse properties to eliminate private mortgage insurance and reduce appraisal risk exposure. USDA loans offer zero down payment for qualifying properties and borrowers, but the rural residential classification requirements eliminate most horse properties with significant equestrian infrastructure. Portfolio lenders financing non-conforming horse properties typically require 25 to 35 percent down. Private lenders require 30 to 40 percent. Seller financing down payments are negotiated and can range from 10 percent to whatever the seller requires. Farm credit lenders such as Farm Credit Services of America typically require 20 to 25 percent for rural residential or agricultural horse property loans. The more specialized or income-producing the property, the higher the required equity position. Buyers should also account for closing costs, which on rural horse property transactions often run higher than standard residential closings due to survey requirements, well and septic inspections, and title complexities.

Key Takeaways

  • Conventional loans may allow 5 to 10 percent down but lender overlays commonly require 20 percent.
  • Portfolio lenders typically require 25 to 35 percent equity.
  • USDA offers zero down but strict classification requirements eliminate most equestrian properties.
  • Private lenders require 30 to 40 percent and do not base decisions on income or credit score.
