Can You Finance Horse Property With a Conventional Loan?

Conventional loans are the most common financing vehicle for horse property purchases and can work well when the property meets standard residential underwriting criteria. Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac guidelines permit financing of rural residential properties with acreage, barns, and outbuildings provided the property is classified as residential, not agricultural or commercial. The determining factors are use, classification, and appraisal support. If a property is owner-occupied as a primary residence and the horses are kept for personal use rather than business income, it often qualifies as residential for underwriting purposes. Appraisers must identify comparable sales within a reasonable distance, typically within a one-mile radius expanded as needed, to support the purchase price. When comparables are scarce — common in rural equestrian markets — appraisers may apply acreage adjustments or use time-adjusted sales, which introduces value risk. Conventional loans also carry acreage guidelines that affect eligibility. Fannie Mae requires the residential portion of the property to remain typical for the area, and excess land that cannot be separated may or may not contribute to appraised value depending on comparable support. Borrowers pursuing conventional financing for horse property should work with lenders experienced in rural lending and ensure the appraisal assignment goes to a rural-qualified appraiser familiar with the local equestrian market.

Key Takeaways

  • Conventional loans are available for horse property classified as residential.
  • Owner-occupied, non-income-producing use is the baseline qualifier.
  • Comparable sales must support the appraised value.
  • Rural-experienced lenders and appraisers significantly reduce underwriting risk.
