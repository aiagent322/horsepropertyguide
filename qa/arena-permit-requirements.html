Can You Build a Riding Arena Without Permits?

Whether a riding arena requires a permit depends on its construction type, size, and location, and the applicable county or municipal code. In most Arizona counties, a completely open, unroofed arena with compacted dirt footing and a perimeter fence does not require a building permit because it is classified as a land improvement rather than a structure.

However, setback requirements for fencing and grading may still apply, and some jurisdictions require a grading permit for significant earthwork associated with arena construction. Covered arenas with a permanent roof structure — metal pole barns, wood frame, or tensioned fabric — are universally treated as structures requiring a building permit, setback compliance, and in some cases, a site plan review.

The permit requirement for covered arenas is triggered by the permanent attachment of a roof, regardless of whether the sidewalls are enclosed. Electrical work within any arena — lighting, outlets, panel boxes — requires an electrical permit regardless of whether the structure itself triggered a building permit.

Buyers purchasing property with an existing covered arena should verify that the structure was permitted and that a final inspection was completed. Unpermitted covered arenas can require retroactive permits, structural upgrades to meet current code, or in extreme cases, demolition orders. Confirming permit history through the county building department before purchase eliminates this risk.

Permit Requirements by Arena Type in Arizona

Arizona's permit requirements for riding arenas vary by county, structure type, and size. Open arenas without any overhead covering — simple fenced enclosures with native or engineered footing — generally do not require a building permit in most Arizona counties. However, grading permits may be required for significant earthwork, and some jurisdictions require fencing permits for arenas with perimeter fencing above a certain height. Buyers should confirm local requirements with the applicable county or municipal building department rather than assuming that an open arena is unconditionally permit-free.

Covered arenas trigger building permits in all Arizona jurisdictions once a permanent roof structure is attached. The roof material, whether metal, fabric, or composite, does not affect the permit requirement — it is the permanent attachment and roofing of the structure that triggers the permit. Fabric shade structures attached to permanent posts may or may not require permits depending on their size and the attachment method; shade cloth stretched over existing fencing posts without new foundations is treated differently than a formal shade structure with engineered footings. Enclosed arenas with walls in addition to a roof have the same permit requirements as barns and must meet applicable building codes for structural integrity, electrical, and fire safety.

What Happens When an Arena Is Unpermitted

Unpermitted covered arenas are a frequent finding in horse property transactions, particularly on properties built out before aggressive enforcement of building codes in rural Arizona counties. The consequences for buyers depend on the county, the structure's age, and the lender's requirements. Some counties have limited enforcement capacity in rural areas and may not proactively identify unpermitted structures. However, if the structure is identified — through a permit search, an appraiser's notation, or a neighbor complaint — the county can issue a compliance order requiring retroactive permitting, structural modification to meet current code, or demolition.

Lenders may exclude unpermitted arenas from the appraised value of the property, which affects the loan-to-value calculation and can create an appraisal shortfall. Buyers who plan to finance a property with an unpermitted covered arena should discuss the issue with their lender and appraiser before committing to the purchase. In some cases, the seller can obtain a retroactive permit before closing. In other cases, the parties must negotiate a price reduction that accounts for the cost of bringing the structure into compliance. Buying an unpermitted arena without addressing the issue at closing transfers the compliance risk and cost entirely to the buyer.

Key Takeaways

  • Open, unroofed arenas with dirt footing typically do not require building permits in Arizona.
  • Covered arenas with permanent roofs require building permits in all Arizona jurisdictions.
  • Electrical work within arenas requires electrical permits regardless of structure type.
  • Unpermitted covered arenas create retroactive compliance risk that affects financing and resale.
