Do Counties or Cities Control Horse Zoning?

Jurisdiction over horse zoning depends on whether a property is located within an incorporated city or town or within unincorporated county territory. Incorporated municipalities — cities and towns with their own governing charters — adopt and enforce their own zoning codes independently of the county.

Unincorporated areas fall under county jurisdiction and are governed by the county's zoning ordinance. This distinction is critical for horse property buyers because the same parcel address can appear to be in one jurisdiction while actually being governed by another.

A property with a Scottsdale mailing address may be in unincorporated Maricopa County if it falls outside Scottsdale's city limits, and vice versa. The two zoning codes can differ substantially on permitted horse density, setback requirements, and allowed equestrian uses.

In Arizona, most rural equestrian land is in unincorporated county territory and governed by county zoning. Properties within city limits in communities like Scottsdale, Gilbert, or Queen Creek are subject to municipal codes that may be more restrictive. When a city annexes previously unincorporated land, the zoning classification may change, potentially eliminating horse-keeping rights that existed under county zoning. Buyers in areas experiencing rapid growth should investigate annexation history and any pending annexation proposals before purchasing horse property.

Key Differences Between County and City Horse Zoning in Arizona

The most important practical difference between county and city horse zoning in Arizona is permissiveness. Unincorporated county areas generally have more accommodating zoning frameworks for horse-keeping than incorporated cities and towns. County agricultural and rural residential zones often allow horses by right at specific density limits without additional approvals. City zoning codes, which must balance equestrian use against residential neighbor expectations, frequently impose more restrictive density limits, stricter setback requirements, additional operational conditions, and broader CUP requirements. A buyer who finds a property unsuitable in city limits due to horse restrictions may find a comparable property two miles away in unincorporated county territory that permits the same use without a CUP.

Annexation is a risk that buyers in unincorporated areas near city boundaries should evaluate. When a city annexes adjacent unincorporated territory, the annexed parcels become subject to the city's zoning code rather than the county's. Existing land uses — including horse-keeping — may be grandfathered as legal nonconforming uses, but new horses, new structures, and changes in use may be required to comply with the city's more restrictive code. Buyers purchasing near active annexation zones should research the city's annexation plans and assess whether their intended equestrian use would be adversely affected if the property were annexed during their ownership.

How to Determine Which Jurisdiction Governs Your Property

The definitive way to determine whether a specific horse property is in an incorporated municipality or in unincorporated county territory is to look up the parcel on the county assessor's website and review the situs address and jurisdiction fields. Most Arizona county assessor websites identify the governing jurisdiction for each parcel. The Maricopa County Assessor, for example, identifies whether a parcel is within a specific municipality or in unincorporated Maricopa County. The distinction is not always evident from the mailing address — a property with a Scottsdale, AZ address can be in the city of Scottsdale, the town of Paradise Valley, or unincorporated Maricopa County depending on the specific location.

Once the governing jurisdiction is identified, buyers should contact that jurisdiction's planning department directly to confirm the zoning classification, permitted uses, and any special conditions that apply to horse-keeping on the specific parcel. This conversation typically takes less than 30 minutes and provides authoritative information that listing sheets, agent representations, and online zoning maps cannot reliably supply. Investing this time before making an offer prevents the most common and expensive horse property purchase mistake — buying a parcel that does not permit the intended use.

Key Takeaways

  • Incorporated cities and towns control zoning within their limits; counties control unincorporated areas.
  • A property's mailing address does not determine which jurisdiction controls its zoning.
  • City and county codes can differ substantially on equestrian use, density, and setbacks.
  • Annexation of county land by a city can eliminate existing horse-keeping rights.
