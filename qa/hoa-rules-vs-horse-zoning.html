Do HOA Rules Override Horse Zoning?

HOA covenants, conditions, and restrictions (CC&Rs) are private legal agreements that operate independently of government zoning and can impose restrictions more stringent than what the zoning code allows. When a property is within an HOA, both the zoning code and the CC&Rs govern the property simultaneously.

Zoning establishes the minimum public standard; CC&Rs can restrict uses further. If county zoning permits horses on a parcel but the HOA's CC&Rs prohibit livestock, the HOA restriction is enforceable against the homeowner.

This means a buyer could purchase an agriculturally zoned parcel that legally allows horses under county code but be prohibited from keeping horses by the HOA's governing documents. HOA enforcement of CC&Rs is handled through civil action, fines, and in some cases, liens — separate from any government zoning enforcement.

Buyers must review the full CC&R document, not just a summary or MLS disclosure, to confirm whether horses are permitted. Relevant provisions may appear in sections titled agricultural use, animals, livestock, or nuisance. Some HOAs in equestrian communities explicitly permit horses and specify density limits, facility requirements, and trail access rights within the CC&Rs. Buyers should obtain a full set of governing documents — CC&Rs, bylaws, and any supplemental rules — and have them reviewed before finalizing a purchase on any horse property within an HOA.

How HOA Enforcement Works on Horse Property

HOA enforcement of CC&R provisions is a civil matter handled between the association and the property owner, not through government agencies. When an HOA determines that a member is violating animal-keeping restrictions, it typically sends a notice of violation, gives the owner a cure period, and then imposes fines if the violation continues. Fines can accumulate and be recorded as liens against the property. In Arizona, HOAs have the right to place liens on properties for unpaid fines and assessments, and these liens can affect the owner's ability to refinance or sell. The HOA can also pursue the owner in civil court to enforce the CC&Rs, which can result in injunctive relief requiring the removal of horses from the property.

The severity of HOA enforcement depends on the specific association, the language of the CC&Rs, and the prevailing attitude of the board and membership. Some HOAs in equestrian communities actively encourage horse-keeping and have CC&Rs specifically structured to protect that use. Others in communities that have transitioned from rural to suburban character may actively enforce provisions that restrict animals. Buyers should not assume that an HOA's current enforcement approach will continue — board composition changes, and a new board that takes a stricter interpretation of the CC&Rs can create compliance obligations that did not exist under prior management.

Due Diligence Steps for HOA Horse Properties

Buyers evaluating horse property in an HOA-governed community should take several specific due diligence steps before closing. First, obtain the full CC&R document and any recorded amendments — not just the summary provided at closing — and read the animal-keeping provisions in their entirety. Second, request confirmation in writing from the HOA management company or board that horses are permitted under the current governing documents and what the specific restrictions are. Third, review the HOA's enforcement history through meeting minutes if available, to assess whether horse-keeping restrictions have been actively enforced against current or prior residents. Fourth, confirm whether any variances or special approvals have been granted to current residents that might not transfer to a new owner.

In Arizona, sellers are required to disclose the existence of an HOA and provide governing documents as part of the purchase disclosure process. Buyers should review these documents during the inspection period — when the purchase contract typically allows cancellation — rather than at closing. Discovering that an HOA prohibits horses on the day of closing creates a difficult situation with limited remedies. The due diligence period exists precisely to allow buyers to identify and evaluate restrictions like CC&Rs that affect the intended use of the property.

Key Takeaways

  • HOA CC&Rs are private restrictions that operate independently of government zoning.
  • CC&Rs can prohibit horses even when county zoning permits them.
  • HOA enforcement is civil, not governmental, and can include fines and liens.
  • Buyers must review the full CC&R document, not summaries, before purchase.
