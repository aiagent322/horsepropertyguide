Do HOA Rules Override Horse Zoning?

HOA covenants, conditions, and restrictions (CC&Rs) are private legal agreements that operate independently of government zoning and can impose restrictions more stringent than what the zoning code allows. When a property is within an HOA, both the zoning code and the CC&Rs govern the property simultaneously. Zoning establishes the minimum public standard; CC&Rs can restrict uses further. If county zoning permits horses on a parcel but the HOA's CC&Rs prohibit livestock, the HOA restriction is enforceable against the homeowner. This means a buyer could purchase an agriculturally zoned parcel that legally allows horses under county code but be prohibited from keeping horses by the HOA's governing documents. HOA enforcement of CC&Rs is handled through civil action, fines, and in some cases, liens — separate from any government zoning enforcement. Buyers must review the full CC&R document, not just a summary or MLS disclosure, to confirm whether horses are permitted. Relevant provisions may appear in sections titled agricultural use, animals, livestock, or nuisance. Some HOAs in equestrian communities explicitly permit horses and specify density limits, facility requirements, and trail access rights within the CC&Rs. Buyers should obtain a full set of governing documents — CC&Rs, bylaws, and any supplemental rules — and have them reviewed before finalizing a purchase on any horse property within an HOA.

Key Takeaways

  • HOA CC&Rs are private restrictions that operate independently of government zoning.
  • CC&Rs can prohibit horses even when county zoning permits them.
  • HOA enforcement is civil, not governmental, and can include fines and liens.
  • Buyers must review the full CC&R document, not summaries, before purchase.
