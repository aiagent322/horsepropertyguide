Shared Road and Access Disputes

Shared road and access arrangements are common on rural horse properties and represent a persistent source of dispute that affects daily operations and long-term property value. Many rural parcels access public roads through easements that cross neighboring land, and these arrangements — regardless of how amicable they appear at the time of purchase — can become contentious when ownership changes, use patterns change, or neighbors disagree about maintenance obligations. An access easement that allows a buyer to cross a neighbor's land may be legally sufficient but practically difficult to enforce if the neighbor blocks access, fails to maintain the shared road surface, or installs gates that limit passage. Horse property owners require road surfaces capable of supporting horse trailers, which are heavier and wider than standard passenger vehicles. Shared roads that are adequate for passenger use may be inadequate for trailer access, and disagreements about the road's required maintenance standard are common. In some cases, shared road agreements include maintenance cost-sharing provisions that buyers inherit and are legally obligated to fund. Buyers must review any access easement documentation before closing, confirm the legal right is adequately described and recorded, assess the physical condition of the shared road, and evaluate the relationship with the neighboring landowner whose property the easement crosses. Access disputes are litigation-prone and can result in temporary or permanent loss of practical property access if not resolved through legal action.

Key Risks

  • Shared road easements can become contentious when ownership changes on either side.
  • Neighbors can block or impede access physically even when the easement is legally valid.
  • Shared road surfaces may not meet the load requirements for horse trailers without upgrades.
  • Access disputes are litigation-prone and can temporarily disrupt or permanently complicate property access.
