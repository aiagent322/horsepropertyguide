HOA Restrictions on Horse Property

Homeowners association CC&Rs can prohibit horses, limit their number, restrict equestrian facilities, or impose facility standards that significantly increase the cost of horse-keeping — all independently of what the zoning code permits. A buyer who relies on zoning confirmation alone and fails to review the full CC&R document can purchase an agriculturally zoned parcel with a barn and corrals and still be legally prohibited from keeping a single horse by the HOA's governing documents.

HOA restrictions on horses appear in sections covering animals, livestock, agricultural use, nuisance, or accessory structures. Summaries and MLS disclosures frequently omit or misstate animal restrictions.

The only reliable source is the complete, recorded CC&R document, which the buyer must review before removing contingencies. HOA enforcement of animal restrictions is handled through civil action.

Fines accumulate, and in egregious cases, the HOA can place a lien on the property. Equestrian HOA communities — which exist in Scottsdale, Cave Creek, and other Arizona markets — explicitly permit horses and often include trail systems and equestrian infrastructure, but even these communities impose specific rules on stall setbacks, manure management, and the number of horses per parcel. Buyers must read the actual governing documents, not rely on verbal representations from sellers, agents, or HOA management companies.

What Arizona HOA CC&Rs Actually Say About Horses

HOA restrictions on horses appear in several sections of CC&R documents and are rarely consolidated in a single, clearly labeled provision. Animal restrictions may appear under sections titled "animals," "livestock," "nuisance," "agricultural use," or "accessory structures." A CC&R document that prohibits "livestock" may or may not include horses in that definition — the governing documents' definitions section controls the interpretation, and horses are sometimes specifically excluded from livestock prohibitions in equestrian communities and sometimes explicitly included. Buyers must read both the substantive restrictions and the definitions carefully to understand what the CC&Rs actually say about horses, not what a summary or agent representation suggests.

Equestrian community HOAs — common in Scottsdale, Cave Creek, and other Arizona horse property corridors — typically include CC&Rs that specifically protect equestrian use while imposing facility standards. These standards may require minimum fencing specifications, mandate specific manure management practices, set setback requirements for equestrian structures that differ from county requirements, establish rules about trailer parking, or require maintenance of trail easements that cross the property. Buyers purchasing in established equestrian HOA communities should review these standards as operational requirements that affect the cost and character of horse-keeping on the property, not just as restrictions to be avoided.

HOA Enforcement and Dispute Resolution

HOA enforcement of animal restrictions in Arizona follows a defined statutory process governed by the Arizona Planned Community Act and Arizona Condominium Act. HOAs must provide written notice of a violation, a reasonable opportunity to cure, and a formal hearing process before imposing fines. Fines for continuing violations are capped under Arizona statute, though the accumulation of daily fines can still reach significant amounts over time. HOAs can record a lien on the property for unpaid assessments and fines, and in some cases can pursue judicial foreclosure of that lien — a severe consequence that underscores the seriousness of HOA enforcement in Arizona.

Buyers who believe an HOA's enforcement action is improper have the right to request an internal hearing and, if unsatisfied, to pursue dispute resolution through Arizona's HOA ombudsman program or civil litigation. The HOA ombudsman — administered by the Arizona Department of Real Estate — provides a lower-cost alternative to litigation for resolving disputes about HOA authority and enforcement practices. However, the best protection against HOA enforcement problems is thorough due diligence before purchase — reading the governing documents, confirming horse-keeping is permitted, understanding the applicable standards, and selecting a property in a community where the HOA's equestrian stance aligns with the buyer's intended use.

Key Risks

  • CC&Rs can prohibit horses even when county zoning permits equestrian use.
  • Animal restriction provisions are frequently omitted from MLS disclosures and summaries.
  • HOA enforcement includes fines and liens that accumulate and follow the property.
  • Equestrian HOA communities permit horses but impose their own specific operational restrictions.
