Water Rights

Water rights are legal entitlements to use water from a specific source — a well, river, stream, canal, or irrigation district — and they are separate from land ownership.

In Arizona, an appropriation state, water rights are governed by the Arizona Department of Water Resources. Groundwater pumped from a private well for domestic and livestock use generally does not require a separate water right outside of Active Management Areas. Surface water from rivers and streams does require a permitted water right. For horse property buyers, water rights are a critical due diligence item because insufficient or legally uncertain water access limits how many horses can be sustained and affects both property value and financing eligibility.

Arizona operates under the doctrine of prior appropriation for surface water, meaning water rights are allocated based on seniority — older rights have priority over newer rights during periods of shortage. This doctrine, often summarized as first in time, first in right, means that a horse property with a junior water right may face curtailment of its water supply during dry years when senior rights holders claim their full allocation. Buyers purchasing properties with surface water rights should review the priority date, permitted use, and annual volume of the right before closing, as these factors determine the reliability of the water supply.

Groundwater in Arizona's Active Management Areas — the Phoenix, Tucson, Prescott, Pinal, and Santa Cruz AMAs — is regulated by the Arizona Department of Water Resources. Within these areas, new wells require permits, annual pumping may be limited, and long-term groundwater availability is governed by the state's assured water supply program. Properties outside AMAs have fewer restrictions on well use, but groundwater is not unlimited even where unregulated. Buyers should review whether a property's water use is within or outside an AMA and understand the applicable regulatory framework before purchase.

Water rights issues on horse properties are most acute on larger operations that require significant volumes of water for livestock, irrigation, and facility maintenance. A small personal-use horse property on a domestic well outside an AMA faces few water rights complications. A large boarding or training facility dependent on surface water or located within an AMA faces more complex regulatory compliance. Buyers of larger operations should engage a water rights attorney to review water rights documentation and assess supply reliability as part of their due diligence.

Key Points

  • Water rights are legal entitlements to use water, separate from land ownership.
  • Arizona requires well registration within Active Management Areas.
  • Surface water use from rivers and streams requires a permitted water right from ADWR.
  • Water rights availability directly affects horse-keeping capacity and property value.
