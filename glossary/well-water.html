Well Water

Well water refers to groundwater accessed through a drilled or bored well on the property. A private well is the preferred water source for horse properties in rural Arizona because it provides a continuous, on-site supply for both residential and livestock use.

Well yield — measured in gallons per minute — determines whether a well can support a horse operation alongside residential demand. Horses require approximately 10 to 15 gallons of water per day, with higher consumption during hot weather.

Low-yield wells may be adequate for residential use but insufficient for equestrian use. Lenders and appraisers assess well yield as part of property evaluation. Well registration and pump test documentation should be reviewed by buyers before closing.

Well yield is the most important performance metric for a horse property well. Yield is measured in gallons per minute and indicates how quickly the well can deliver water. A well with a yield of 1 to 3 gallons per minute may be adequate for a small household but insufficient when combined with equestrian water demand. Horses drink 10 to 15 gallons per day each, and a barn operation with automatic waterers, wash racks, and arena dust suppression can require several hundred gallons per day in addition to residential household use. Buyers should obtain a pump test — conducted by a licensed well contractor — that measures yield under sustained pumping conditions, not just instantaneous flow rate.

Well registration and documentation requirements vary by location in Arizona. Within Active Management Areas, wells must be registered with the Arizona Department of Water Resources and may be subject to annual pumping limits. Outside AMAs, domestic and livestock wells generally do not require separate water rights, but registration provides a legal record of the well's existence and priority. Buyers should confirm that the well is properly registered, that permit records are available, and that the well has been maintained according to Arizona Department of Environmental Quality standards for potable water wells.

Well maintenance history is a critical due diligence item. Buyers should ask for documentation of the last well inspection, pump service records, and any water quality testing results. Water quality testing should check for coliform bacteria, nitrates, arsenic, and other contaminants common in Arizona groundwater. Lenders may require a water quality test as part of the loan approval process. A well that delivers adequate yield but produces water with contaminants requiring treatment adds ongoing operational cost and may require capital investment in filtration or treatment systems.

Key Points

  • Private wells provide on-site, continuous water and are preferred for rural horse properties.
  • Horses require 10 to 15 gallons per day; well yield must support both residential and equestrian use.
  • Lenders and appraisers evaluate well yield as part of collateral assessment.
  • Buyers should review well registration and pump test records before closing.
