Horse Arena

A horse arena is an enclosed or open area specifically designed for riding, training, or exercising horses. Arenas range from small round pens used for groundwork to large rectangular enclosures used for roping, reining, cutting, jumping, and other competitive disciplines.

They may be open-air with natural dirt or engineered footing, partially covered with shade structures, or fully enclosed with permanent roofing. Open arenas without permanent roofing typically do not require building permits in Arizona, while covered arenas require building permits, setback compliance, and structural inspections.

Arena size, footing type, and drainage quality directly affect the property's equestrian utility and market value. Unpermitted covered arenas create compliance and financing risk.

Arena size standards vary by equestrian discipline. A standard dressage arena is 20 by 60 meters. A western pleasure or reining arena typically runs 150 by 300 feet or larger. Roping arenas require additional length for run-up and scoring. Buyers should evaluate arena dimensions against their intended disciplines before purchase, as expanding an arena after the fact requires significant earthwork and may trigger permit requirements for additional covered structures.

Permit status is a critical issue for covered and enclosed arenas. A covered arena with a permanent roof requires a building permit in all Arizona jurisdictions. Unpermitted covered arenas create the same compliance and financing risks as unpermitted barns — lenders may exclude them from appraised value, and county building departments can require retroactive permitting or removal. Open-air arenas without permanent overhead structures typically do not require permits, though the earthwork involved in grading and footing installation may require grading permits in some jurisdictions.

Arena drainage is a frequently overlooked but critical factor. A poorly drained arena becomes unusable within hours of significant rainfall. In Arizona's monsoon climate, arena drainage failures result in extended periods of unusability during the summer riding season. Buyers should assess the slope, base preparation, and drainage provisions of any arena before purchase, and should ask the seller about arena performance during monsoon rain events. A well-drained, properly graded arena built on a compacted base is significantly more valuable and lower-maintenance than one built on untreated native soil.

Key Points

  • Horse arenas range from open round pens to large enclosed competition facilities.
  • Open arenas typically do not require building permits; covered arenas do.
  • Arena footing and drainage quality directly affect equestrian utility and value.
  • Unpermitted covered arenas create retroactive compliance and financing risk.
