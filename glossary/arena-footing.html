Riding Arena Footing

Riding arena footing is the surface material used in a horse arena to provide traction, cushioning, and support for horses during work. Common footing materials include native dirt, decomposed granite, sand, rubber, and engineered blends of sand and fiber or rubber.

The right footing depends on the discipline, climate, and drainage characteristics of the arena. In Arizona's dry climate, native decomposed granite is frequently used for outdoor arenas.

Poor footing — hard-packed dirt, overly deep sand, or surfaces that become slick when wet — increases the risk of injury to horses and riders. Appraisers assess arena footing condition when evaluating equestrian improvements, and deteriorated footing reduces contributory value.

The choice of footing material has significant long-term cost and maintenance implications. Native decomposed granite compacts over time and may require periodic scarification to restore cushion and drainage. Sand footing requires regular dragging and may need moisture management to prevent dust, particularly in Arizona's dry climate. Engineered footing blends using rubber, fiber, or polymer additives are more expensive to install but typically require less ongoing maintenance and provide more consistent performance across weather conditions. Buyers evaluating a horse property with an existing arena should assess the footing type, depth, drainage, and condition as part of their inspection.

Footing failures — compaction, contamination, or poor drainage — are expensive to remediate. Full replacement of arena footing on a standard 100-by-200-foot arena can cost $5,000 to $25,000 or more depending on material selection and base preparation. Buyers who overlook footing condition during inspection may face significant capital expenditures shortly after purchase. Appraisers note footing condition when evaluating equestrian improvements, and poor footing can reduce the contributory value of an arena regardless of its size and structure.

In covered or enclosed arenas, footing management also involves dust suppression, which is both a horse health issue and a neighbor relations issue in Arizona's dense equestrian communities. Properties subject to conditional use permits may have specific conditions regarding dust control from arena use. Buyers should review any CUP conditions attached to the property before closing to understand their ongoing compliance obligations related to arena operations.

Key Points

  • Arena footing provides traction and cushioning for horses during work.
  • Common materials include decomposed granite, sand, rubber, and engineered blends.
  • Footing selection depends on discipline, climate, and arena drainage.
  • Deteriorated or inadequate footing reduces the appraised contributory value of an arena.
