Hauled Water

Hauled water is water transported to a property by truck and stored in above-ground tanks or cisterns for residential and livestock use.

It is a legal and common practice in rural Arizona where private wells are absent, yield is insufficient, or utility water service is unavailable.

Hauled water is typically purchased from municipal fill stations or private suppliers and stored in polyethylene or galvanized tanks sized to meet daily demand. While legal, hauled water has significant limitations for horse property buyers: it introduces ongoing cost and supply dependency, may not satisfy county building permit requirements, and reduces financing eligibility and appraised value compared to properties with private wells or utility water service.

The practical limitations of hauled water for horse property are significant. A horse requires approximately 10 to 15 gallons of water per day under normal conditions, with higher consumption during hot weather or heavy exercise. A small operation with four horses requires 40 to 60 gallons per day — roughly 300 to 420 gallons per week — in addition to residential household demand. Large storage tanks sized at 2,500 to 5,000 gallons can buffer against delivery frequency, but buyers must account for ongoing hauled water costs as an operational expense, not a one-time investment.

Hauled water can also affect financing. Some lenders and USDA underwriters view hauled water as a risk factor in property evaluation, particularly when there is no clear path to well development or municipal water connection. Buyers financing with conventional loans should confirm whether their lender requires documentation of an adequate water supply that hauled water alone may not satisfy. USDA loan programs have specific water adequacy standards that hauled water may or may not meet depending on the program and underwriter.

Buyers evaluating a hauled water property should investigate whether well development is feasible on the parcel — drilling costs, expected yield based on neighboring wells, and permit requirements from the Arizona Department of Water Resources. In some areas, well development is cost-prohibitive or legally restricted within Active Management Areas. Understanding the long-term water strategy for the property is essential before purchase, particularly for buyers planning to expand their horse operation.

Key Points

  • Hauled water is legally permitted throughout Arizona for livestock and domestic use.
  • It introduces ongoing cost, supply dependency, and operational complexity.
  • Lenders may decline or discount properties relying solely on hauled water.
  • Hauled water typically does not satisfy county building permit water supply requirements.
