Agricultural Zoning

Agricultural zoning is a land use designation that allows farming, ranching, livestock-keeping, and related operations as primary permitted uses.

It is the most favorable classification for horse property because it typically imposes no density limits on horses, allows barns and agricultural structures as permitted uses, and supports commercial equestrian operations such as boarding and training without requiring additional permits.

In Arizona, agricultural zoning designations include AG, A-1, A-2, and General Agricultural. Parcels with agricultural zoning may not qualify for conventional residential financing, often requiring farm credit or portfolio loan products instead. Agricultural classification also affects property tax — land assessed as agricultural typically carries a lower tax rate than residential land.

One important distinction for buyers is that agricultural zoning and agricultural tax status are not the same thing. A parcel can be zoned agricultural while being taxed at a residential rate, or it can carry an agricultural valuation for tax purposes while being zoned residential. These classifications are administered by different agencies — zoning by the county planning department, and property tax classification by the county assessor. Buyers should verify both independently, as a change in use can trigger a tax reclassification and result in back-assessment of taxes at a higher rate for the prior three years in Arizona.

Financing on agriculturally zoned horse property requires a lender experienced in rural lending. Conventional Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac loans are available for agriculturally zoned parcels when the property is used as a primary residence and horses are kept for personal use rather than income. If the parcel generates farm income or is assessed as an agricultural operation, it may be reclassified as a farm by the underwriter and become ineligible for residential financing. Portfolio lenders and USDA loan programs handle agricultural properties differently and are often better options for buyers purchasing larger or income-producing parcels.

Buyers evaluating agriculturally zoned horse property should confirm the specific zoning designation directly with the county planning department, as the permitted uses, density rules, and setback requirements vary significantly between AG, A-1, A-2, and Suburban Ranching classifications. Adjacent agricultural parcels under different sub-classifications may have materially different rules, and the property's specific designation governs what is permitted regardless of what neighboring properties allow.

Key Points

  • Agricultural zoning is the most permissive classification for horse operations.
  • It typically allows horses, barns, and commercial equestrian use without additional permits.
  • Agricultural-zoned property may not qualify for conventional residential financing.
  • Agricultural classification reduces property tax assessment in most states including Arizona.
